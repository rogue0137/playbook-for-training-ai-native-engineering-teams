# Week 12 — Pilot Reflection & Leadership Summary

## Focus

Twelve weeks ago you took a baseline survey and wrote down how you
currently work. This week you look back at that picture and compare
it to what's true now.

The entire program was built on one idea from [outcome engineering](https://o16g.com/):
humans define the destination, agents explore the paths. Twelve weeks
of practice — no vibe coding, context engineering, LEARNINGS.md, code
reading, design review, test generation, pair programming, voice coding,
building something real, two days without AI — all of it was training
one thing: your ability to define the destination clearly enough that
an agent can be useful getting there.

This week you say what changed. What you're keeping, what you're
dropping, and what you'd tell the next cohort. Those answers —
specific, honest, grounded in twelve weeks of actual work — are what
leadership needs to make good decisions about where this goes next.

One more thing before you close out: this week you also build a
multi-agent orchestration pipeline. Not as a separate exercise — as
the last entry in your LEARNINGS.md. You've spent twelve weeks learning
to orchestrate AI. This week you make that visible at the system level.

### 1:30–3:30 — What Twelve Weeks Was Actually About

- The program was built on one idea from [outcome engineering](https://o16g.com/):
  humans define the destination, agents explore the paths
- Every week was a different expression of that:
  - Month 1 — discipline: building the habits that keep the human
    in the driver's seat (no vibe coding, context engineering,
    LEARNINGS.md)
  - Month 2 — orchestration: applying those habits across the full
    SDLC (code reading, design review, test generation, pair
    programming)
  - Month 3 — examination: testing the limits of your own
    orchestration (voice coding, building something real, two days
    without AI)
- The arc wasn't "learn to use AI" — it was "learn to stay the
  engineer while using AI"
- Screen share a simple visual timeline of all twelve weeks here
  if you have one

### 3:30–6:00 — The Orchestration Exercise

> This is the most direct expression of [outcome engineering](https://o16g.com/)
> in the entire program. Complex outcomes require sequenced agent steps.
> This exercise makes your own sequencing visible — at the system level,
> not just the prompt level.

- The task: design and document a multi-agent pipeline for something
  real in your workflow
- What that means in practice:
  1. Identify a multi-step task you do regularly that involves more
     than one kind of AI output — e.g., read code → generate tests
     → review output → write summary
  2. Map it: what does each agent step receive, produce, and hand
     off to the next step?
  3. Build it — or build the first two steps if the full pipeline
     is too large for one session
  4. Document it in LEARNINGS.md as a reusable pattern
- This isn't about complexity — it's about making your orchestration
  thinking explicit and leaving it somewhere you can find it
- Example: _"I built a pipeline that takes a Jira ticket, generates
  an implementation plan, scaffolds the code structure, and produces
  a test outline — three agent steps, each with a defined input
  and output"_
- Call it out: _"This is what outcome engineering looks like at the
  system level — you defined the destination, you broke the path
  into steps, and the agents executed each one"_

### 9:30–10:00 — Why This Matters Beyond the Pilot

- The engineers who get the most out of AI long-term aren't the
  ones who use it the most — they're the ones who use it
  intentionally, know its limits, and keep their own judgment sharp
- That's [outcome engineering](https://o16g.com/) as a practice,
  not a principle: humans choose the destination, agents explore
  the paths, and the human stays responsible for where you end up
- Your feedback shapes what AI training looks like at Crunchyroll

### Video best practices

- Name specific weeks and activities by name in the recap — don't
  just say "Month 2"; say "the week you pair programmed with AI"
  and "the week you turned Copilot off for two days"; specificity
  makes participants feel the distance they've traveled.
- Be honest about what surprised you running the program; it models
  the honesty you're asking for in the reflection form.
- Add captions for async viewing.
