# Week 9 — Voice Coding

## Focus

Month 3 starts here. Month 1 built the habits. Month 2 expanded where
AI shows up in the workflow. Month 3 is about pushing on the assumptions
— starting with the most basic one: that typing is how you talk to AI.

Voice prompting is a different physical act than typing, and that
difference matters. When you type a prompt, you edit as you go — you
slow down, second-guess, compress. When you speak, you explain. You
give context you wouldn't have thought to write. You also expose how
unclear your thinking actually is when you can't hide behind the
backspace key.

This week participants try it — at least two sessions, real tasks, with
the template to capture what changed. The question isn't whether voice
is better. The question is what voice reveals about how you currently
specify intent.

## Learning Objectives

By the end of Week 9 participants will:

- Have used voice prompting for at least two real coding sessions and
  compared the experience to typed prompts
- Know whether speaking changes how they specify intent, decompose
  problems, or provide context to an agent
- Have a clearer picture of their own prompting habits — specifically
  where voice exposes gaps that typing conceals

### 1:30–3:30 — Why Voice?

- Typed prompts: deliberate, edited, compressed — you cut context
  before you send
- Voice prompts: conversational, faster, less filtered — you explain
  more than you meant to
- The research question this week: does speaking change how you
  decompose a problem or specify intent?
- Theory: speaking tends to make intent more explicit — you narrate
  what you want instead of shortcutting to what you think will work
- It also exposes unclear thinking fast — speaking a prompt out loud
  often surfaces gaps you'd miss typing. If it takes a few tries to say
  it clearly, that's normal; the act of trying is part of the practice.
  _(Some people find writing clearer than speaking — that's valid too,
  but voice is worth trying at least once)_
- Practical concerns: open offices, privacy, feeling awkward — all
  valid; there are workarounds

### 3:30–6:00 — LIVE DEMO: Voice Coding

- Screen share with audio on
- Task: _"Add error handling to an authentication function"_
- Show typed version first:

  ```
  Add error handling to this auth function for invalid token,
  expired token, and missing token cases.
  ```

- Now show voice version — speak it naturally:

  _"Hey, I have this authentication function that currently throws
  generic errors. I want to add specific error handling for three
  cases: invalid token, expired token, and missing token. Each
  should return a clear message that tells the user what went wrong.
  Can you help me refactor this?"_

- Call out the difference: the voice version gave more context
  without trying to — the explanation came naturally
- That's the experiment: not whether voice is better, but what it
  reveals about your intent specification habits
