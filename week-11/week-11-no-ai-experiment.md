# Week 11 — No AI Experiment

## Focus

Ten weeks of working with AI. This week you work without it — for
two days.

Not as a punishment and not because AI is bad. Because after ten
weeks of using it constantly, it's almost impossible to tell the
difference between two things that feel identical from the inside:
AI accelerating work you understand, and AI covering for work you
don't. Two days without it is the fastest way to find out which
one is actually happening.

Some tasks will just feel slower. That's fine — speed is a legitimate
thing AI gives you, and noticing the absence of it is data. But some
tasks will feel genuinely harder in a different way — like you've
forgotten how to start, or you're not sure what the right answer
even looks like without AI to confirm it. That's the more interesting
finding. That's what this week is for.

## Learning Objectives

By the end of Week 11 participants will:

- Have spent two full working days without code-generation AI and
  documented what they noticed
- Know the difference — for their own work — between where AI was
  masking missing understanding and where it was legitimately
  accelerating it
- Have an honest picture of their AI dependency patterns heading
  into the final week of the pilot

## Participant Time Commitment

| Activity                   | Time           |
| -------------------------- | -------------- |
| Instruction video          | 10 min         |
| Two no-AI work days        | normal workday |
| End-of-day reflections × 2 | ~15 min        |
| Weekly summary reflection  | ~15 min        |
| Share findings in Slack    | 5–10 min       |
| **Total**                  | **~45–50 min** |

> The two no-AI days are your normal work — they don't add time,
> they replace how you'd normally work. The reflection is what's new.

## Important Notes for Program Lead

This is the week most likely to produce friction. Know what to
expect and how to respond.

**Expected reactions:**

- _"This is dumb — why would I handicap myself?"_
- _"I can't finish my sprint commitments without AI"_
- _"I feel really slow and frustrated"_

**How to respond:**

- This is about awareness, not performance — struggling is the point,
  not a failure condition
- It's two days, not a new policy
- Honest reflection is worth more than looking good — the data from
  this week is more valuable if people are real about what they found

**If someone genuinely can't do two full days:**

- Two half-days or one full day is the minimum — they still need to
  feel the contrast to get anything out of it
- Offer this proactively to anyone who flags sprint pressure before
  Tuesday; don't wait for them to give up mid-week

## Welcome Video Script (10 min max)

> _The video script uses "you" to address participants directly — that's
> intentional. Write and record it as if you're speaking to them, not
> about them._

### 0:00–1:30 — Welcome + Week 10 Recap

- Pull 2–3 demo highlights here — what people built, what surprised
  them, how long it actually took
- Common theme from build weeks: when you scope it small and break
  it down, a week is a lot of time
- This week is the opposite of last week in one specific way: last
  week AI was the tool; this week you're finding out what the tool
  was doing for you that you didn't notice

### 1:30–3:30 — Why Two Days Without AI?

- Ten weeks of constant AI use creates patterns that are hard to
  see from the inside
- Two distinct things can feel identical when you're in them:
  1. AI accelerating work you understand — fast, you could do it
     without AI, it would just take longer
  2. AI masking work you don't — you can use the output but you
     couldn't produce it or evaluate it without AI's help
- Both are real. Neither is shameful. But knowing which one is
  happening matters — especially if you're the one responsible
  for the code in production
- Two days without AI is the fastest way to find out which you're
  actually dealing with

### 3:30–6:00 — LIVE DEMO: What to Notice During No-AI Days

- Walk through three realistic scenarios without screen-sharing code:

  **Scenario 1: Writing a new function**
  - With AI: prompt, review, modify
  - Without AI: write from scratch or find examples in the codebase
  - What to notice: did you know how to start, or were you waiting
    for AI to suggest an approach?

  **Scenario 2: Debugging a failing test**
  - With AI: paste the error, ask what's wrong
  - Without AI: read the error, trace the stack, use the debugger
  - What to notice: was it harder, or just slower?

  **Scenario 3: Understanding unfamiliar code**
  - With AI: ask for an explanation
  - Without AI: read the docs, search the codebase, ask a teammate
  - What to notice: did you miss AI's speed, or did you find you
    learned something you wouldn't have otherwise?

- Call it out: _"Slower is not the same as stuck. They're different
  findings and both belong in your reflection."_

### 6:00–8:00 — The Experiment This Week

- Screen share the no-AI reflection template
- Pick two days — announce them in Slack
- What's off for those days:
  - GitHub Copilot autocomplete
  - ChatGPT / Claude for code generation
  - Cursor AI features
- What's still fine:
  - Google, Stack Overflow, documentation
  - Reading the codebase directly
  - Asking teammates
  - AI for non-code tasks if you genuinely need it
- End-of-day reflection each day — 10 minutes, not an essay
- Weekly summary at the end — where did AI mask understanding
  versus where was it just making you faster?

### Video best practices

- Be honest on camera about your own AI habits — if you think
  there are places where AI has been masking gaps for you, say so;
  it gives participants permission to be honest in their reflections.
- Keep the three scenarios grounded and specific; vague examples
  don't land.
- Add captions for async viewing.
